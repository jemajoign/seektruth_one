## Pythagorean Distance vs Measured Distance
If it is not possible to measure the distance accurately on at least one of the sides with wrenches, how did I know that the distance was a never-ending series of digits (refinements)? I am of course using the Pythagorean theorem, which tells me that, with the original wrench, the diagonal size across from me will be $\sqrt{2^2+4^2}=2\sqrt{5}$.

![Pythagorean Theorem](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Pythagorean_theorem_abc.svg/250px-Pythagorean_theorem_abc.svg.png?_=20080524152636)

The purpose of a theorem is to describe reality as accurately as possible. If we can never measure an irrational value, how is this theorem useful in describing reality? The thing is, if you make your measurements more and more accurate, using smaller and smaller mini-wrenches, you find that your measurements converge to the ever-narrowing distance value specified by the irrational number's never-ending digits. So, the theory has merit. 

Of course, you can only make your wrenches so small. Once you get too small, the physical world does not give us predictable values for distance. Planck length ($1.616 \times 10^{-35}$ meters) is considered the limit of how small we can get with measuring distances due to quantum mechanics. So if you were using a wrench and it was a quarter of a meter long, after 34 decimal places, the digits of the irrational number would not be of much use to you. On the other hand, you could start with something much longer than a wrench, like a rod that reached from the earth to the moon. In that case, up to 43 decimal places would be helpful. Your rod would have to stretch to about the nearest star to find need for more than 50 digits. To use 60 digits, you'd have to stretch as far as the knowable universe. So needless to say, you're not going to find a scenario where you will need even the 100th digit, much less infinite digits. 

One side of the Pythagorean (ideal) triangle may be an irrational multiple of another. Can a measured length be irrational? Since you can only measure accurately up to a *finite* number of digits, even with [the most accurate measuring equipment in the world](https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/video/ligo20160211v6#:~:text=The%20effects%20of%20the%20gravitational%20waves%20on,only%201/1000th%20the%20size%20of%20a%20proton.), you can never measure an irrational distance. Every measured length is therefore treated as a rational multiple of a very small unit, like the smallest lines on your measuring tape. Of course, the table you are measuring never *perfectly* aligns with even that smallest unit. So how can we say that the measured distance is rational? If no smallest unit captures the length, should we consider it irrational? It can be considered rational only in this sense -- at some point we must give up the quest for greater precision and be satisfied with an **approximation**. Perhaps it is because we hit the Planck length. More likely, we will have hit other *much* larger limits than that, such as that objects expand and contract with temperature and have uneven surfaces, making more precise measurements pointless. One way or the other, we are forced to make every measurement of length an approximation. And if we are approximating the length, we can choose to approximate it to the nearest rational number or the nearest irrational number, depending on which suits our purposes better.
