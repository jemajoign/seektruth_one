## Introduction

When I was in college, my mom developed a fondness for a cat, which eventually gave birth to a few kittens. Observing this cat taking care of its young, my mom commented, "See the love of a mother!" This troubled me at the time because I understood love as being something distinctly human, as opposed to the instinctual activity of the cat. Yet, the truth is, anyone who has a pet dog or cat and observes its behaviors carefully will see evidence of something like love, something like guilt or shame, and even instances of something like altruism.

I do not believe that animals love in the way that humans love. But that there is something like love there, who can deny?

When we take the concept of "love", which is human, and map it certain feelings and behaviors in animals, something may trouble us. After all they are not the same thing. They are not at the same level. The animalistic dimensions of "love" are found in human being but not vice versa. In other words, there is a reduction of the concept of love. Such a reduction comes with the onset of fear. "Are you saying that love is just animal instinct?"

This is where the concept of a map (i.e. a mapping) can be helpful. Once you recognize that you are employing a map, you do not mind the reductions. In fact, you might enjoy them. They bring to light all kinds of symmetries in disparate "regions" of reality. They allow the wisdom of one reality to applied to another. Before going further, let's explain what we mean by a map.

## A Plain-Old Map
Think about any plain-old map, the kind that no one uses anymore because of Google Maps. At some point, someone had to go out there and survey the vast, three-dimensional world and try to capture it on a single page. By doing so, they have produced a handy, miniature version of reality that you can carry in your pocket.

A map is a fascinating thing. The length of your pinky may be one hundred miles on the map. Think about how much information is "compressed" there. In fact, it is not compressed. It is reduced. Information is lost. When you walk down a street, there are innumerable details to notice: every tree and leaf, the lamp posts, the potholes, the storm drain, etc. All of this information is lost in the process of making the map. If your city vanished and someone tried to recreate your street looking at the map, they would have to arbitrarily decide on details like that because the map failed to capture that information.

Even though a map loses information, it still represents the reality accurately. At least, this is what allows us to differentiate between an accurate map and an erroneous one. For example, if the map says you will, when driving north on such and and such street, see the church first, then the library, then the McDonald's, if you saw instead the church and then McDonald's and then the library, you would know that the map is inaccurate. In other words, a true map loses information without losing accuracy or a certain fidelity to the original.
